UX Persona Review — SEO Page Builder

Vendingpreneurs website · scoped to /admin/pages + published output · 2026-06-10 · http://localhost:3000 (branch ux-review-fixes, dev auth bypass) · 15 personas + 2 evidence verifiers
How to read this report.

We tested the page builder the way real people would: a robot walked every screen, clicked every safe button, filled every form, and attempted 7 realistic tasks ("journeys") end-to-end — creating, publishing, previewing, scheduling a page, and so on. Then 15 simulated reviewers ("personas" — a grandmother, a power user, a screen-reader user, a phone-only user, an angry customer, etc.) each independently studied the recordings and flagged everything that confused or blocked them. Two verifier agents then re-checked every serious claim against the screenshots before it was allowed into this report.

P0 = fix first: it breaks or badly damages a core task, or nearly every reviewer hit it.  P1 = serious: damages trust or shuts out a group of users.  P2 = real friction people will work around.   P3 = polish. "11/15 personas" means 11 of the 15 reviewers independently flagged the same problem — a strong signal it's not a matter of taste.

The Story in Three Sentences

The builder is genuinely good at the creative part: picking blocks, editing content on the canvas, and previewing a draft all work well — the share-a-preview-link flow is better than what Webflow or WordPress offer. But the moment a page needs to go live, the tool stops communicating: publishing is a guessing game of one-at-a-time error messages, scheduling gives no confirmation at all, redirects can never be undone, and abandoned edits silently turn into ghost draft pages. In short: the machine does the work correctly, but it doesn't tell you the truth about what it just did — and that's exactly where users need certainty most.

Key Evidence

P0 — Fix These First

P0-1 · Publishing is a guessing game: requirements are revealed one error at a time

P0Editor14 of 15 personas
What happens
When you finish a page and try to publish it, the Publish button is greyed out. The only explanation is a small orange note beside it — for example Fix SEO title. You fix that and click Save… and instead of unlocking, a new note appears: Fix content 1 · Cta Label. Fix that, and another appears: Fix content 1 · Destination URL. The tool knows the full list of requirements the entire time — it just never shows it. Our test run needed four rounds of "fix one thing, discover the next" before the button unlocked. The messages are also written in the system's internal naming ("content 1", "Cta") rather than words a person uses.
Why it matters
You can never tell how close you are to done. Each "fixed it!" moment is punished with a new demand, which is exactly the pattern that makes people abandon software. This is the single most-flagged issue in the review and the reason the publish journey scored 1.2/5.
"It made me fix one hidden riddle after another, in words I didn't understand, and one field wasn't even on the page."
— Betty (78, non-technical)
"This is where I'd literally throw my phone."
— Zoe (16)
Fix: Replace the single rotating message with a visible checklist of everything still required before publishing — in plain language ("Add a headline to the Hero section"), each item clickable so it jumps to (and opens) the right field. The user should see the list shrink as they work.
Evidence: blocker chip · next blocker appears · publish-chain.json

P0-2 · Scheduling a page to publish later gives zero confirmation it worked

P0Editor15 of 15 personas — unanimous
What happens
There's a "Scheduled publish" box where you can pick a date and time for the page to go live automatically. We typed tomorrow at 12:00 and clicked "Save draft". The toast said "Draft saved." — and the date box was empty again, showing dd/mm/yyyy, --:-- --. Nowhere on the entire screen does it say "This page will publish on 11 June at 12:00". The only clue that a schedule exists at all is that a checkbox labelled "Cancel scheduled publish" quietly appears further down.
Why it matters
Scheduling is an unattended action — you set it and walk away. If you can't see proof it was accepted, you either don't trust it (and publish manually anyway) or you do trust it and discover the failure when a client asks why the page never went live. One of our personas, Karen, was written as "an angry user whose scheduled page never went live and she doesn't know why" — and the review reproduced her exact scenario. Every single reviewer flagged this.
"I have no evidence my site is queued to go live. For an unattended action, that's disqualifying."
— Rachel (skeptical marketing director)
Fix: After saving, keep the chosen date in the box and show a persistent status — "Scheduled to publish Wed 11 Jun, 12:00 PM (Pacific Time)" — in the Publish Status card, with a clear Cancel button next to it.
Evidence: empty field after save · journeys.md schedule-publish

P0-3 · A field you MUST fill to publish doesn't exist anywhere on the page

P0Editor12 of 15 personas
What happens
One of the publish requirements is Fix content 1 · Destination URL — the hero section's button needs a link target. Reasonable rule. The problem: you can scroll the entire editor top to bottom and there is no field called "Destination URL" anywhere. It only exists inside a hidden popup: hover the hero block → click the "⋮ Block actions" button → choose "Edit settings" → and there, in a modal, is "CTA destination URL". The error message never mentions any of that. Our automated tester — which methodically clicks everything — took three attempts to find it; a human gets no such patience.
Why it matters
This is a dead end disguised as an instruction. The user is told to fix something they cannot find, which reads as "the software is broken" and ends sessions. Several personas independently called it the point where they'd give up or phone someone.
"The message gives no location; without that hint I cannot find the field at all."
— Yuki (non-native English speaker)
Fix: Two options, ideally both: (1) make the error message a link that opens the block-settings popup with the field focused; (2) show the destination field inline on the canvas right next to the button label it belongs to.
Evidence: the hidden modal · filled → publish unlocks

P0-4 · After you publish, the "Confirm publish" box stays open and asks you to publish again

P0Editor12 of 15 personas
What happens
Clicking Publish shows a yellow confirmation card: "Publish this page? This will make this draft visible at /resources/…" with Cancel and Confirm buttons. Good. You click "Confirm publish". A green "Changes published." message appears — but the yellow card does not close. It stays on screen, buttons still active, now reading "This will replace the current live version…". So the screen simultaneously says "you published it" and "do you want to publish it?"
Why it matters
The user's very next thought is "did it actually work, or do I need to click again?" Clicking again is harmless today but the user doesn't know that — and a UI that contradicts itself at the moment of highest stakes (changing the live website) torches trust in everything else it says.
"The success toast fires while the scary 'this will replace the live version' card stays open, so I can't tell if it worked."
— Karen (angry user)
Fix: When publishing succeeds, dismiss the confirm card and swap in a success state: "✓ Published — Open live page". (That link also fixes issue #14.)
Evidence: both states on screen at once

P0-5 · Redirects can be created but never edited or deleted — typos are permanent

P0Redirects screen11 of 15 personas
What happens
A redirect forwards an old web address to a new one (e.g. so links to a renamed page don't break). The Redirects screen has a form to create them and a table listing them — and that's it. The table rows have no edit button, no delete button, no menu. Whatever you type, the moment you click "Create redirect", it is permanent as far as this screen is concerned.
Why it matters
Redirects are exactly the kind of thing people typo (they're raw URLs), and a "301" redirect specifically tells browsers and Google "this move is permanent — remember it", so they cache it aggressively. A mistyped redirect can silently send visitors to the wrong place for months with no way to stop it from the admin. We know it's real because this review's own three test redirects are now stuck in that table — cleaning them up requires going into the database directly.
"I came here because I made a redirect with a typo — and this screen shows me the typo and won't let me fix it."
— Karen (angry user)
Fix: Add Edit and Delete (with a confirm step) to each row. A "disable" toggle would be even safer than delete for accidental-removal recovery.
Evidence: the table — five columns, no actions

P0-6 · Leave the editor without saving: no warning — and a ghost draft is silently created

P0New-page editor10 of 15 personas
What happens
We opened "Create page", typed a title, and then clicked back to the pages list without saving — the classic "phone rang, came back later" moment. Two things went wrong at once. First, there was no "you have unsaved changes" warning — the editor just let us walk away. Second, and stranger: verification showed the typed content wasn't thrown away — the tool had silently saved it as a brand-new draft page, with its own web address, sitting in the drafts list. Nobody ever pressed Save. (Our probe page, literally titled "Unsaved data-loss probe (do not save)", appeared as draft #5.)
Why it matters
Either behaviour alone is bad; together they're worse, because the user can't predict which one they got. Someone who wanted to keep their work believes it's gone. Someone who wanted to discard a false start has unknowingly littered the site with a half-named draft page that has a publishable URL. Over months this produces a drafts list full of ghosts nobody remembers creating.
"Two places where my work vanished or where I couldn't tell if something saved."
— Betty
Fix: Intercept navigation away from an editor with unsaved changes: "Save draft / Discard / Keep editing". And never persist a draft to the database before the user explicitly saves (or, if autosave-on-create is intentional, say so on screen: "Draft created automatically").
Evidence: back on the list, ghost draft visible, no warning shown

P1 — Serious

P1-1 · The tool gives three different explanations for why publishing is blocked — at the same time

P1Editor8 of 15 personas
What happens
In one screenshot — one moment in time — the status chip says Fix SEO title, the readiness panel says Add a hero headline before publishing., and the SEO title field itself says Leave blank to use page headline — i.e. "this field is optional". Three surfaces, three stories. The actual problem was the empty headline, but we (like a real user) fixed the SEO title first because that's what the chip demanded. The chip's wording even drifts between "Fix SEO title" and "Improve SEO title" in different states.
Why it matters
When messages contradict each other, users stop believing any of them — and they waste effort fixing the wrong thing, which compounds the P0-1 whack-a-mole feeling.
Fix: Drive every blocker display from one source of truth, and name the actual missing thing ("Add a hero headline"). If a field is optional, never name it in a blocker.
Evidence: all three messages in one frame

P1-2 · Autosave can fail and the editor doesn't tell you

P1Editor1 persona (developer) — verified real
What happens
The editor saves your work automatically as you type and shows "Saved automatically · 3:45 PM". During testing, one of those background saves failed with a network error. The editor showed nothing — no banner, no change to the "saved" indicator. The failure was only recorded in the browser's hidden developer console: seo page autosave failed TypeError: Failed to fetch.
Why it matters
An autosave indicator is a promise: "you don't need to worry about saving." If it can silently break, the user keeps typing for an hour believing they're protected, closes the laptop, and the work is gone. Only one reviewer caught this (the one simulating a developer with the console open) — that's precisely why it's dangerous: normal users have no way to see it. Severity comes from the damage, not the count.
Fix: When an autosave fails, show a visible warning — "Couldn't save — retrying… / Save manually" — and retry automatically. Flip the "Saved" indicator to a "Not saved" state.
Evidence: journey-results.json console log (verified verbatim by verifier-b)

P1-3 · A blind user can't find out why the Publish button is disabled

P1EditorAccessibility
What happens
Sighted users at least see the small orange blocker note next to the greyed-out Publish button. A screen reader (software that reads the page aloud for blind users) just announces "Publish, button, dimmed" — the reason isn't programmatically attached to the button, so it's never read out. The user knows publishing is blocked but has no way to learn why.
Why it matters
Combined with P0-1, a screen-reader user isn't playing whack-a-mole — they can't even see the mole. Publishing becomes literally impossible without sighted help.
Fix: Associate the blocker text with the button (aria-describedby) so it's announced together with it, and announce blocker changes via a live region.
Evidence: verification-b.json cluster M; editor ARIA snapshot

P1-4 · The pages list shows status as unlabelled coloured dots

P1Pages list11 of 15 personas
What happens
Each page row has two columns — "Readiness" and "Status" — whose entire content is a small coloured dot: red, amber, green, blue, or grey. There are no words next to the dots and no legend anywhere on the page. To learn what a dot means you must hover your mouse over each one and read a tooltip, one at a time. (For screen-reader users the dots do carry hidden text names — so this is a visual problem, not a total accessibility block; the verifiers checked.)
Why it matters
The one thing a manager wants from this list — "which pages are live, which are in trouble?" — can't be answered at a glance, which is the entire point of a status column. Colour-only signals also fail for the ~8% of men with colour-vision deficiency.
"Page status is not glanceable from the list."
— Victoria (executive with 30 seconds)
Fix: Put the word next to the dot ("● Published", "● Needs work") or add a one-line legend above the table.
Evidence: the list view

P1-5 · Screen readers can't hear the names of the page blocks in the editor

P1EditorAccessibility — "serious" automated violation
What happens
The blocks on the canvas (Hero, FAQ, …) are labelled for assistive technology using a technique that HTML doesn't actually allow (aria-label on a plain span). The result: screen readers are required to ignore those labels, so the building blocks of the page are nameless to a blind user. The industry-standard automated checker (axe) flags this at "serious" severity.
Why it matters
Without block names, a screen-reader user can't tell which section of the page they're in while editing — navigation of the canvas becomes guesswork.
Fix: Move the label onto an element that's allowed to carry one (the block's focusable wrapper or a heading), or give the span an appropriate role.
Evidence: axe-results.json — aria-prohibited-attr, span[aria-label="Hero block"], span[aria-label="FAQ block"]

P1-6 · There is no undo for drafts until the page has been published once

P1Editor9 of 15 personas
What happens
The editor has a "Revision history" panel — a list of earlier versions you can restore. But while you're still drafting a new page, that list is empty and stays empty: saving a draft does not create a restore point. The empty-state text confirms it: "Revisions appear after publishing, library refreshes, or draft restores." So during the riskiest period — building the page, deleting and re-adding blocks, experimenting — there is no safety net. Delete the wrong section and save? It's gone.
Why it matters
People assume a CMS with a "Revision history" panel is protecting them. It isn't, precisely when they're most likely to make a destructive mistake. Every major competitor (WordPress, Webflow, Framer) snapshots drafts.
"That's the safety net I came looking for, and it isn't there until it's too late."
— Karen
Fix: Create a revision on every manual "Save draft" (or every N minutes of changes), so drafting has restore points from the first save.
Evidence: the empty revisions panel during drafting

Verification Honesty Box

Every P0 and P1 claim above was re-checked against the screenshots by two independent verifier agents before making this report. Five claims were softened: the redirects and unsaved-exit issues were downgraded from "completely blocks the task" to "critical"; the mobile-editor, colour-dots and block-naming issues from "critical" to "serious". One claim was corrected outright: the unsaved-exit issue originally said typed work was lost — the screenshots prove it was actually silently saved as a ghost draft, which is its own kind of problem. One persona claim (that the status dots are invisible to screen readers) was found to be wrong — the dots do have hidden text names — so that issue is recorded as visual-only.

Withdrawn after owner correction: the review originally flagged "scheduling uses Pacific Time (America/Los_Angeles)" as a wrong-timezone defect, on the assumption that Vendingpreneurs is an Australian business. The owner has confirmed it is a US West Coast business — Pacific Time is the correct timezone, so that finding (11 personas flagged it) is withdrawn entirely. The schedule field's clear timezone label is in fact good practice. The silent-save problem with scheduling (P0-2) is unaffected and stands.

Leftover test records (manual cleanup): 3 test redirects that cannot be deleted via the UI (/resources/ux-old-test-1781071681847, /resources/ux-old-test-1781071765035, /resources/ux-explore-old-1781071765035) — remove via the database when convenient. Archived (harmless, in the Archived tab): “UX SEO Review Test 1781071765035 v2”, its “Copy of…” duplicate, and “Unsaved data-loss probe (do not save)”. One test governance comment on the archived page. Preview token auto-expires 2026-06-13.
